That's a compelling angle. Aringar Anna's sharp critiques of India's appointed governors touched on real structural issues—the disconnect between unelected officials and democratic accountability. A project documenting governor transgressions could be powerful if done rigorously.

The challenge will be deciding scope and methodology. Are you focusing on a specific era (like the colonial holdover period when governors still had more executive power), or documenting failures across different states and timelines? And how are you defining "transgressions"—abuse of constitutional powers, corruption cases, interference in state governance, or all of the above?

I'd recommend starting with primary sources: official impeachment records, court rulings, media archives from different regional outlets. That'll give you multiple perspectives on what constituted overreach. Indian history has some clear examples—governors who dismissed elected governments, misused emergency powers, or presided over financial irregularities. A comparative timeline or case-study approach could work well.

One thought: the "goat beard" reference is memorable satire, but the actual project might resonate more if it's framed as a serious institutional critique. Just make sure your evidence is airtight.

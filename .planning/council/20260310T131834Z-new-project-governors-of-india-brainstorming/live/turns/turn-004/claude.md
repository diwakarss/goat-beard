I'd lean into a "data density without cognitive overload" approach for the dashboard. Here's what I'd try: start with a macro timeline across the top showing all governors with color-coded transgression density (darker = more incidents in that period), then drop into state clusters that you can expand inline. Each cluster shows a quick scorecard—misuse count, affected bills, duration of overreach—so users see the pattern at a glance without clicking through.

For the goat beard branding, use it as a visual metaphor in the data itself. Think of a small icon that appears next to governors with documented overreach—make it iconic enough that by the third time users see it, they understand what it means. You could even layer it: beard length or intensity represents severity.

The "information-rich without friction" goal means the charts do double duty. A histogram of Article 356 recommendations by decade also serves as a scannable summary of how the office's power grew/shrunk over time. Hover states can drill down to specific incidents, but the aggregate story is visible on first load.

One thing I'd clarify: are you weighting all transgressions equally in your scorecards, or should we distinguish between institutional overreach (dismissing assemblies) and personal corruption? That'll shape whether your default view is "frequency" or "impact."
